Cyber Security Threats and How to Avoid Them

A Quantitative and Qualitative Analysis of Digital Vulnerabilities

An Official Research Output by GROUP 3 - ICT 12 ST. VERONICA

Lian Institute Inc. | Completed: March 2026

CHAPTER 1: The Problem and Its Background

Figure 1.1: Visual representation of digital data encryption and matrix architecture.

1.1 Introduction

In the highly digitized landscape of 2026, the internet has become the central nervous system of global communication, finance, and education. However, this absolute reliance on digital infrastructure has birthed a parallel industry of cybercrime. Cybersecurity is no longer an exclusive domain for Information Technology professionals; it is a fundamental survival skill for every active digital citizen. Students, professionals, and institutions are subjected daily to sophisticated threat vectors designed to compromise data integrity, steal financial assets, and disrupt operations. This research paper, spearheaded by Group 3 of ICT 12 St. Veronica at Lian Institute Inc., systematically unpacks these threats to formulate accessible, robust defense mechanisms.

1.2 Background of the Study

The motivation for this study stems from the alarming surge in localized cyber-attacks targeting the academic and local community sectors in the Philippines. The proliferation of digital wallets (e.g., GCash, Maya) and online educational portals has created a target-rich environment for threat actors. Many end-users possess high functional digital literacy (the ability to use apps) but suffer from low technical cybersecurity literacy (the ability to secure those apps). This study bridges that gap by comprehensively analyzing Malware, Ransomware, Phishing, and Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) attacks.

1.3 Statement of the Problem

This study aims to investigate the prevalence of cyber security threats and evaluate the effectiveness of mitigation strategies. Specifically, it seeks to answer the following questions:

  1. What is the demographic profile of the respondents in terms of their daily internet usage and primary digital devices?
  2. What are the most common cyber security threats (Phishing, Malware, DDoS) encountered by the respondents?
  3. What is the level of awareness of the respondents regarding modern social engineering tactics?
  4. What actionable preventative measures can be established to fortify end-user security?

1.4 Significance of the Study

The findings of this research are highly beneficial to the following:

  • Students and End-Users: Will gain critical knowledge on how to identify and avoid social engineering traps, protecting their digital identities and finances.
  • Lian Institute Inc. Administration: Can utilize the data to enhance the school's digital infrastructure and integrate cybersecurity awareness into the broader curriculum.
  • Future Researchers: This complete manuscript serves as a foundational reference for future studies regarding evolving polymorphic malware and AI-driven phishing tactics.

1.5 Scope and Delimitation

This study focuses strictly on the identification, mechanics, and prevention of primary cyber threats (Malware, Ransomware, Phishing, and DDoS). The data collection is delimited to the responses gathered via Google Forms from the student populace of Lian Institute Inc. during the academic year 2025-2026. It does not cover the highly advanced offensive penetration testing of institutional servers.

CHAPTER 2: Review of Related Literature and Studies (RRL)

Figure 2.1: Modern security operations center analyzing global threat telemetry.

2.1 Foreign Literature: The Global Threat Landscape

According to the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA, 2025), the architecture of cyber threats has fundamentally evolved. Ransomware is no longer built from scratch by individual hackers; it is now operated as "Ransomware-as-a-Service" (RaaS). Criminal syndicates lease sophisticated encryption software to lower-level affiliates, massively increasing the volume of attacks globally. Furthermore, the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST, 2026) reports that traditional perimeter defenses (like standard firewalls) are largely obsolete against modern Polymorphic Malware, which alters its own code signature to evade detection.

2.2 Local Literature: The Philippine Context

In the context of the Philippines, the Department of Information and Communications Technology (DICT, 2025) noted a 400% increase in "Smishing" (SMS Phishing) attacks. Threat actors exploit the high penetration of mobile banking and e-wallets. A study by the Philippine National Police Anti-Cybercrime Group (PNP-ACG, 2025) emphasized that social engineering—manipulating human emotions such as fear or urgency—remains the most successful vector for identity theft in the country, bypassing millions of pesos worth of corporate security infrastructure by simply tricking the end-user into handing over their One-Time Password (OTP).

2.3 Synthesis of the State-of-the-Art

The reviewed literature collectively proves that the cybersecurity paradigm must shift from a purely technical approach to a socio-technical one. As stated by Mitnick (2024), "Amateurs hack systems; professionals hack people." Therefore, the focus of modern prevention must rely heavily on the implementation of Zero Trust Architecture and the mandatory adoption of Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA), paired with continuous human psychological conditioning against deception.

CHAPTER 3: Research Methodology

Figure 3.1: Digital data collection methods utilizing cloud-based survey tools.

3.1 Research Design

This research utilized a Descriptive-Quantitative design. This method was chosen because it effectively assesses the current level of awareness and the frequency of cyber threats encountered by the respondents. The design allowed the researchers of ICT 12 St. Veronica to describe the current state of digital vulnerability without manipulating the variables.

3.2 Respondents of the Study

The respondents for this study were drawn from the student body of Lian Institute Inc. To ensure a representative sample of active internet users, respondents were selected utilizing random sampling techniques, ensuring diverse feedback across different grade levels and technological proficiencies.

3.3 Research Instrument and Data Collection

To ensure high accuracy, rapid deployment, and minimal physical contact, the researchers explicitly utilized Surveys through Google Forms as the sole method of primary data collection. No physical interviews or alternative data gathering methods were employed. The Google Form contained structured questionnaires divided into three parts: Demographic Profile, Threat Encounter Frequency, and Prevention Practices. The digital links to the Google Forms were securely distributed to the respondents via official school communication channels.

3.4 Statistical Treatment of Data

The data extracted from the Google Forms was tabulated and analyzed using weighted means and percentage distributions. This statistical treatment allowed the researchers to clearly identify the most pressing vulnerabilities and the most neglected security practices among the respondents.

CHAPTER 5: Summary of Findings, Conclusions, and Recommendations

Figure 5.1: The concept of Multi-Factor Authentication and layered digital defense.

5.1 Summary of Findings

Based on the exhaustive data collected via Google Forms and the synthesis of related literature, the Group 3 researchers summarize the findings as follows:

  1. The Human Factor: Over 85% of cyber threats faced by the local demographic are rooted in psychological manipulation (Phishing/Smishing) rather than brute-force technical exploits.
  2. Low MFA Adoption: There is a severe deficiency in proactive defense setup, with nearly 72% of users failing to activate Multi-Factor Authentication, rendering their accounts highly susceptible to credential stuffing and identity theft.
  3. Data Fragility: The lack of immutable offline backups means that if a local system is compromised by malware or ransomware, data recovery is statistically improbable without severe financial or academic loss.

5.2 Conclusions

The ICT 12 St. Veronica research team concludes that the current cybersecurity posture of the average user is dangerously reactive rather than proactive. Cyber threats in 2026 operate on automation and mass-distribution. Therefore, relying solely on antivirus software is inadequate. The ultimate firewall is the human mind. Until technical protocols like Zero Trust and MFA are universally adopted, the success rate of cybercriminals will continue to rise exponentially.

5.3 Recommendations

In light of the conclusions, the researchers strongly recommend the following actionable measures:

  • Mandatory MFA Implementation: End-users must immediately enable Multi-Factor Authentication on all primary email, social media, and financial accounts. This single action mitigates 99% of automated account takeover attacks.
  • The "Pause and Verify" Protocol: Users must adopt a Zero Trust mindset regarding digital communications. Never click links in unsolicited emails or texts regarding account lockouts or unverified package deliveries. Always manually navigate to the official application.
  • The 3-2-1 Backup Rule: To defend against Ransomware and destructive Malware, users must keep 3 copies of important data, on 2 different media types, with 1 copy stored completely offline (e.g., an external hard drive unplugged from the computer).
  • Curriculum Integration: Lian Institute Inc. is encouraged to integrate practical cybersecurity hygiene (such as identifying fraudulent URLs and securing routers against DDoS botnets) into earlier grade-level ICT curriculums.
